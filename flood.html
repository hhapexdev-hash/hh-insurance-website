The honest version

What flood insurance covers — and what it doesn't

Covered

  • ✓ The structure: foundation, walls, electrical & plumbing systems
  • ✓ HVAC, water heater, built-in appliances
  • ✓ Permanently installed cabinets, paneling, and flooring
  • ✓ Contents: furniture, clothing, electronics (own policy or rider)
  • ✓ Washer, dryer, and portable AC units
  • ✓ Debris removal after the water recedes

Not covered (NFIP)

  • — Temporary living costs while you rebuild (no ALE)
  • — Most contents below the lowest elevated floor & in enclosures
  • — Pools, fences, decks, docks, and landscaping
  • — Vehicles (that's your auto policy's comprehensive)
  • — Currency, precious papers, and most valuables above sublimits
  • — Mold or moisture damage you could have prevented

Private flood policies can fill several of these gaps (ALE, higher limits, basement contents). That's exactly the comparison we run for you.

Quick answers

Flood FAQ

I'm not in a flood zone. Do I really need this? +

Every home in Florida is in a flood zone — just zones with different letters. A fifth to a quarter of all flood claims come from "low-risk" zones, and preferred-risk pricing there is usually at its cheapest. Low risk is exactly when coverage is a bargain.

NFIP or private flood — which is better? +

Neither, universally. NFIP is standardized and never non-renews you for claims; private can offer higher limits, ALE, faster binding, and sometimes better pricing. We quote both and show you the trade-offs side by side.

How fast can coverage start? +

NFIP typically has a 30-day wait (waived at loan closing). Several private markets can bind in days — but every market freezes new business once a named storm approaches. Buy before the season gets interesting.

Will a flood claim raise my homeowners premium? +

Flood is a separate policy, so a flood claim doesn't hit your homeowners loss history. NFIP can't non-renew you for claims; some private carriers reassess at renewal — another trade-off we'll walk you through.
